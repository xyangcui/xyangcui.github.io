## A brief introduction to midlatitude jets

## Features of changing midlatitude jets

Intensity, latitude, and waviness are the top three characteristics the academy focuses on. From a zonal-mean perspective, both hemispheric jets are projected to strengthen in the future, shift poleward, and become more wavy. For example, Woollings et al. (2023) have detected a poleward shift trend in observations. Zhou, Leung, and Lu (2022) have documented seasonally and regionally dependent jet shifts. They found that westerly jets in the early-summer Asia–Pacific region, the late-winter North American–Atlantic region, and the winter/spring eastern Pacific can shift equatorward.

## Possible mechanisms of changing midlatitude jets

### changes induced by changing background

Two competing mechanisms are commonly discussed. The first is Arctic amplification, which is thought to weaken the surface temperature gradient, especially the baroclinicity over storm-track regions. This reduces eddy activity and tends to shift the jet equatorward. The second is enhanced warming in the tropical upper troposphere, which strengthens the upper-level meridional temperature gradient and tends to shift the jet poleward.

### changes induced by internal dynamics

This view comes from the idea that the jet is a result of eddy–mean flow feedback. Inherently, the jet tends to shift poleward. According to critical-level dynamics, or barotropic Rossby wave propagation, the critical level occurs where $U(\phi_c)=c\cos\phi_c$. As a result, eddies dissipate less on the equatorward side, the poleward eddy momentum flux becomes stronger, and the eddy momentum flux convergence (EMFC) strengthens on the poleward flank of the jet. Therefore, the jet tends to move poleward. This process is sustained by low-level eddies (Zhang et al., 2012) and eddy-driven secondary circulation (Gerber and Vallis, 2007), forming a positive feedback.

Based on this view, Kidston et al. (2010) argued that the eddy length scale will increase (the speed of eddies will decrease), allowing waves to propagate farther away from the jet. This shifts the EMFC farther outside the jet and favors a poleward jet shift.

A second study proposes a different mechanism. It argues that enhanced tropical upper-tropospheric warming (ETUW) can strengthen the jet on its equatorward side, thereby narrowing the distance to the critical level. This leads to stronger poleward eddy momentum flux and eventually shifts the jet poleward. Meanwhile, the closer critical level prevents eddies from propagating farther equatorward, causing deceleration there.
